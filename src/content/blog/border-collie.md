intro: >-
  <span class="dropcap">T</span>here is a particular tilt to the Border Collie's head — a fraction
  of a second before the recall command lands — that no other breed quite manages. Photographers
  chase it. Painters envy it. Our renderer, last summer, was missing it. So we trained on a thousand
  more Collies. This profile is the result: the three plates we now recommend for the breed, the
  photographic angle that catches that tilt, and the gift portraits that ship most often into homes
  that already smelled, faintly, of damp tweed.

distinctive:
  title: "What makes the Border Collie visually distinctive."
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      Three things conspire on canvas. The first is <em>the eye</em> — that low-set, obsessive stare
      bred for crouching at stock, which translates into a portrait that holds the room. Owners often
      comment that the painting looks at them across a kitchen in the way the dog itself does. We have
      learned to overstate it slightly in the source photograph; on the canvas it never quite reads as
      too much.
    - >-
      The second is the <em>asymmetric mask</em>. Black-and-white is statistically the dominant coat —
      about seventy per cent of our Collie commissions — and the mask is almost never centred. That
      off-set black is a gift to portraiture. The painter has something to balance against, and the
      negative space (the white blaze, the white ruff) does work that takes us pages to write up.
    - >-
      The third is the <em>low crouch</em>. Even at rest, a Collie holds half its weight forward. This
      translates to a portrait stance that reads as <em>about to do something</em> — coiled rather than
      relaxed. For a working breed, this is not the problem you might expect: it is exactly the dog its
      owners recognise.

  analysis:
    - >-
      The <em>Oil on Canvas</em> is the recommendation. It is also our most-requested plate and, for
      the Collie, the most demanding — get it wrong and the dog looks like a child's drawing of a wolf.
      Get it right and you have a portrait that will hang for fifty years. The trick is in the muzzle:
      impasto on the bridge, glaze across the cheekbone, the kind of dimensional structure the breed
      deserves.
    - >-
      <em>Watercolour</em> reads soft where the Collie itself is sharp — a contradiction that works.
      Pale washes around the iris, almost-dry brush along the muzzle. For younger dogs (under two),
      before the chest white settles into its adult shape, the medium forgives the asymmetry that age
      will eventually resolve. It is the only style we will gently steer customers towards if their dog
      is under one.
    - >-
      <em>Renaissance</em> is the surprise: the least expected and, for many owners, the eventual
      favourite. The chiaroscuro treatment permits the breed to stop working, briefly, and simply be —
      a state the live dog rarely affords its photographer. We have hung several Renaissance Collies in
      the studio kitchen for exactly this reason.
    - >-
      For the fourth plate — and the one we send most often to memorial commissions — see below. A
      Charcoal study suits the older Collie in a way none of the colour mediums quite manage.

photoTips:
  title: "Photo tips specific to Border Collies."
  plate: { no: "14f", title: "Mid-task, three-quarter", style: "Recommended angle", variant: 0, breed: pointy }
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      The single most useful instruction we give Collie owners is this: <em>photograph the dog at work,
      not at rest</em>. The breed's resting face is closed, neutral, almost guarded; its working face is
      the one you actually live with. A ball mid-air, a sheep in the middle distance, even a recall
      command from the photographer — any one of these produces the head-tilt that defines the portrait.
    - >-
      Lighting: the breed punishes harsh midday sun, which crushes the black mask into a flat shape and
      loses every wrinkle of expression. Overcast, or twenty minutes after sunrise, is the right window.
      Indoors, a north-facing window over the dog's left shoulder catches the mask in the way our
      renderer expects.
