intro: >-
  <span class="dropcap">I</span>f you wanted to put one dog in a field guide to working breeds — one
  specimen, labelled, to stand for the whole idea of a herding dog — it would be a Border Collie.
  The breed is the working animal distilled: no spare parts, nothing decorative, every line of it
  explained by the job. That makes it a wonderful and slightly daunting thing to paint. A Border
  Collie portrait is not really a portrait of a coat or a face. It is a portrait of
  <em>attention</em> — the breed's defining quality — and this profile is the three styles that can
  carry it, including one that treats the Collie exactly as a field guide would.

distinctive:
  title: "What makes the Border Collie visually distinctive."
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      It begins, as every Border Collie does, with the <em>eye</em>. The breed is famous for a
      particular intensity of gaze — "the eye," shepherds call it, the low, focused, almost hypnotic
      stare bred to control stock at a distance — and it is the single most important thing a Collie
      portrait has to carry. Owners do not always have the word for it, but they know instantly whether
      a painting has it. A Border Collie without the eye is just a black-and-white dog. A Border Collie
      with it holds a room.
    - >-
      The <em>coat</em> is the breed's technical demand. The classic Border Collie wears a moderate
      black-and-white coat — a black body and mask broken by a white blaze, collar, chest and feet — and
      that hard contrast is the painter's real work. The black must keep its contour rather than
      collapsing into a void; the white must stay white without blowing out into blank paper. The
      negative space the white markings carve is as much the portrait as the dog itself.
    - >-
      The <em>build</em> is athletic, medium-sized and entirely functional — a dog that even at rest
      carries its weight low and forward, half-crouched, ready to move. There is no such thing as a
      relaxed Border Collie outline; the stance always reads as <em>about to</em>. A portrait should
      keep that coiled quality. A square, settled Collie is a Collie with the job taken out of it.

      <em>look like they own the room</em>. Layered glaze finds blue and umber inside the black so the
      dark coat keeps its form, the white markings hold their brightness against a warm ground, and the
      eye — painted with the depth oil allows — does its quiet, unsettling work. It is the plate we send

      vintage scientific style — fine ink linework, delicate stippling, muted washes on aged cream paper

photoTips:
  title: "Photo tips specific to Border Collies."
  plate: { no: "10f", title: "Three-quarter working stance, eye level, soft overcast light, dog engaged", style: "Recommended angle", variant: 5, breed: pointy, image: "../../assets/blog/border-collie/photo-tips.jpg", alt: "Reference photograph of a Border Collie showing the recommended portrait angle and lighting" }
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      The Border Collie's photographic problem is the contrast, and a camera handles contrast badly. In
      hard, direct light a black-and-white Collie tears in two: the white blaze and chest blow out to
      featureless paper, while the black body and mask crush into a flat, contourless dark. The two
      halves of the dog stop belonging to the same animal. The fix is soft, even, <em>gentle</em> light
      — an overcast sky is close to ideal, or open shade — which a camera can hold at both ends, keeping
      detail in the white and form in the black.
    - >-
      For the rest, photograph the breed as the working dog it is. A three-quarter stance with the
      weight low and forward keeps the coiled outline; eye level is essential, both for dignity and
      because the famous eye only reads when the camera meets it; and the single most useful instruction
      we give is to photograph a Collie <em>engaged</em> — watching a ball, a person, a sheep in the
      middle distance. A Border Collie with nothing to attend to switches the eye off, and the eye is
      the portrait.
