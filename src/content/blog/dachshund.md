intro: >-
  <span class="dropcap">Y</span>ou could identify a Dachshund from its shadow. No other breed we
  paint is so completely <em>its outline</em> — long, low, improbable, a dog drawn by someone who
  refused to lift the pen. That is a portraitist's dream and a portraitist's trap in one. The dream
  is that the silhouette does half the work before a brushstroke is laid. The trap is that a lazy
  portrait stops there, hands you a shape, and calls it a dog. A Dachshund is not a shape. It is a
  shape with one of the boldest faces in the hound group balanced on the end of it, and this profile
  is about painting both.

distinctive:
  title: "What makes the Dachshund visually distinctive."
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      The <em>proportion</em> comes first, because it is the breed. A Dachshund is long where other dogs
      are tall — a deep chest carried low on short, well-boned legs, a topline that runs nearly level
      from the base of the neck to the set of the tail. That outline is so emphatic it can be read as
      decoration, and a good portrait resists that. The length has to feel <em>structural</em> — a
      working build, bred to go to ground — not cartoonish. The line of the back is the spine of the
      picture in every sense.
    - >-
      The <em>head and expression</em> are where the breed earns its second look. The Dachshund face is
      long and tapering, the muzzle fine, the eyes bright, dark and almond-shaped, and the long ears set
      high and hanging close — framing a face that is bold, alert and faintly comic without the dog ever
      intending comedy. There is real terrier-like confidence in a Dachshund's expression, and a
      portrait that softens it into sweetness has missed the animal entirely.
    - >-
      The <em>coat</em> is the variable. Smooth (the most common we paint, and the one most clearly
      defined by its silhouette), long, or wire — each changes the breed's edge. A smooth Dachshund like
      Otto reads as a clean, hard outline; the rich chocolate-and-tan or red coats carry deep, saturated
      colour with little surface detail, which throws the whole portrait, once again, onto shape and
      face.

  analysis:
    - >-
      <em>Geometric</em> is the recommendation, and it is the rare case where the most modern style is
      also the most honest. The Dachshund's long, low form is already a study in clean lines and planes,
      and faceted low-poly treatment honours that shape better than any painterly style can — angular,
      considered, sculptural, the silhouette celebrated rather than softened. It is the plate that looks
      like it was designed, and for this breed that is a compliment.
    - >-
      <em>Pop Art</em> is the bold companion. The Dachshund silhouette is, frankly, built for silkscreen
      — instantly legible, graphically perfect, the kind of shape that works at any size on anything.
      Flat saturated colour and hard outline turn the breed into the icon it half is already. It is the
      liveliest plate, and the one that travels best onto a small format.
    - >-
      <em>Japanese Woodblock</em> is the surprise, and a genuinely beautiful one. The breed's unusual
      proportions suit woodblock's bold linework and compositional simplicity — a long dog reads
      wonderfully across a wide, spare composition, the way a heron or a wave does in the prints the
      style descends from. It is the most artful plate on the list, and the one owners frame most
      carefully.

photoTips:
  title: "Photo tips specific to Dachshunds."
  plate: { no: "5f", title: "Side-on profile, low, from a few steps back", style: "Recommended angle", variant: 5, breed: floppy, image: "../../assets/blog/dachshund/photo-tips.jpg", alt: "Reference photograph of a Dachshund showing the recommended portrait angle and lighting" }
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      The Dachshund's photographic problem is geometry, and it catches almost everyone. A long dog
      photographed close-up, from the front or at an angle, <em>distorts</em> — the near end balloons,
      the far end shrinks, and the breed's whole point, its even, level length, is lost. The single most
      useful instruction we give is to photograph a Dachshund <em>side-on</em>, in true profile, so the
      silhouette reads at its honest proportions. That outline is the portrait; protect it.
    - >-
      Two further things. Step back and zoom in rather than moving close — distance flattens the lens
      distortion that short range exaggerates — and get <em>low</em>. A Dachshund stands only a few
      inches off the ground, and a photograph taken from human height looks down on a foreshortened,
      apologetic dog. Lie down if you have to. Photographed from its own eye level, in profile, at a
      sensible distance, a Dachshund finally looks the way it looks in life: deliberate, low, and
      entirely sure of itself.
