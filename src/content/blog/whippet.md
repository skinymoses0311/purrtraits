intro: >-
  <span class="dropcap">N</span>o breed is so nearly a single line. The Whippet is curve and economy
  — the arched loin, the tucked waist, the long taper of muzzle — and on canvas that economy reads as
  elegance the moment the light is right. Of every breed we paint, the sighthound is the one our
  renderer was almost born knowing. This profile is the short argument for the long dog.

distinctive:
  title: "What makes the Whippet visually distinctive."
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      It begins with <em>the line of the back</em> — that uninterrupted arch from neck to tail that no
      other breed offers so cleanly. A portrait of a Whippet is, more than anything, a portrait of a
      single confident curve. Get the curve right and the rest follows.
    - >-
      Then there is <em>the coat</em>: short, fine, almost a non-coat, which means the painter is
      rendering muscle and bone directly rather than fur. The breed shows its anatomy the way a horse
      does. Light skims along the shoulder and the ribs in a way that flatters every medium we offer.
    - >-
      And finally <em>the stillness</em>. The Whippet at rest is a study in folded geometry — a comma
      on a sofa. Where the Collie is coiled, the Whippet is draped. It is the rare breed that photographs
      better asleep than awake.

  analysis:
    - >-
      <em>Oil on Canvas</em> is the recommendation, as it tends to be — but for the Whippet the reason
      is specific. The medium loves the long muscle of the breed; impasto along the shoulder reads as
      light catching skin, and the result is a portrait that looks lit from within.
    - >-
      <em>Watercolour</em> is the natural home for the fawn and the blue coats, where pale washes do
      most of the work and the white of the paper carries the highlights. For a cream Whippet it is the
      first style we suggest.
    - >-
      <em>Renaissance</em> is the drama plate — chiaroscuro turns the breed's natural elegance into
      something close to sculpture. It is not for every dog, but for a black Whippet against a dark
      ground it is unmatched.

photoTips:
  title: "Photo tips specific to Whippets."
  plate: { no: "15f", title: "Side-on, low angle", style: "Recommended angle", variant: 0, breed: pointy }
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      Photograph the Whippet <em>side-on and low</em>. The breed's whole case is its silhouette, and
      the silhouette is a profile. Get the camera down to the height of the dog's chest and let the
      arch of the back fill the frame.
    - >-
      The fine coat means the breed is unusually sensitive to backlight. A low sun behind the dog rims
      the whole outline in gold — exactly the line we want to paint. Avoid flat front-light, which loses
      the muscle entirely.
