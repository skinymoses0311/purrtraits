intro: >-
  <span class="dropcap">T</span>he German Shepherd holds itself like a dog still, very faintly, on
  duty. Even at rest there is a readiness to the breed — ears up, weight forward, eyes already on
  the door — and that readiness is the entire subject of the portrait. It is also the thing most
  easily lost. A renderer reaching for "good dog" will soften the Shepherd into something kind and a
  little vacant, and an owner will know within a second that the painting is of a different animal.
  The German Shepherd is kind. It is not vacant. This profile is the three styles that keep the
  seriousness intact, and the light that stops the breed's black saddle swallowing the picture.

distinctive:
  title: "What makes the German Shepherd visually distinctive."
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      The <em>bearing</em> comes first, because it is what owners are commissioning whether or not they
      have the word for it. A German Shepherd carries itself with a long, low, ground-covering outline —
      a level back sloping gently to the hindquarters, weight carried forward, a stance that reads as
      <em>attention</em> even in stillness. A portrait that stands the dog square and relaxed has
      painted the body correctly and the animal wrongly. The line of the Shepherd is a working line, and
      it should look like one.
    - >-
      The <em>head and ears</em> carry the expression. The Shepherd head is noble and clean-lined, the
      muzzle strong, the ears large, erect and mobile — and the eyes are the breed's depth: dark,
      almond-shaped, intelligent, and watchful in a way that is alert rather than anxious. There is real
      gravity in a German Shepherd's face. A good portrait treats that gravity as a virtue and resists
      every temptation to warm it into a grin.
    - >-
      The <em>coat</em> is the technical problem. The classic German Shepherd wears a dense double coat
      in a black-and-tan saddle pattern — rich tan over the legs and face, a deep black saddle across
      the back, often a black mask — and the contrast between the two is severe. The tan carries colour
      and light easily; the black saddle, in the wrong hands, becomes a flat dark void with no contour
      at all. Half the craft of a Shepherd portrait is finding the form inside that black.

  analysis:
    - >-
      <em>Oil on Canvas</em> is the recommendation, and for this breed it is barely arguable. The German
      Shepherd has a serious, working presence, and oil is the medium of serious portraiture — depth in
      the coat, weight in the pose, the museum gravity of a painting that expects to be taken seriously.
      Oil also solves the saddle: layered glaze over a warm ground finds blue and umber inside the
      black, so the saddle reads as a coat with contour rather than a hole in the picture. It is the
      plate we send to most Shepherd owners.
    - >-
      <em>Pencil Sketch</em> is the close and characterful second. The breed's dense double coat and
      alert expression are made for detailed graphite and charcoal — the texture of the coat becomes the
      texture of the drawing, and the medium handles the black saddle honestly, as tone rather than
      absence. It is the plate that most rewards a close look, and the one working-dog owners often
      quietly prefer.
    - >-
      <em>Japanese Woodblock</em> is the dramatic outlier. The German Shepherd's standing silhouette —
      that long, alert, unmistakable outline — is exactly the kind of bold shape woodblock composition
      was built around, set against a spare, striking landscape. It is the least traditional plate and
      the most arresting, and it suits an owner who wants the portrait to command a wall rather than
      warm a hall.

photoTips:
  title: "Photo tips specific to German Shepherds."
  plate: { no: "7f", title: "Standing three-quarter, ears up, eye level, soft directional light", style: "Recommended angle", variant: 5, breed: pointy, image: "../../assets/blog/german-shepherd/photo-tips.jpg", alt: "Reference photograph of a German Shepherd showing the recommended portrait angle and lighting" }
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      The German Shepherd's photographic problem has a name owners will recognise: the black dog
      problem, applied to half a dog. The tan photographs easily; the black saddle does not. In flat or
      dim light the saddle collapses into a single dark shape with no contour — the camera simply
      records "black" and stops — and the breed's powerful topline vanishes with it. The fix is light
      with <em>direction and softness</em>: an overcast sky, or open shade, with the light raking gently
      across the dog so it picks out the sheen and the form inside the black coat.
    - >-
      For pose, photograph the Shepherd <em>standing</em>, three-quarter on, so the breed's working
      outline — the level back, the forward weight, the long line — reads clearly. A seated Shepherd
      loses the very thing that makes it a Shepherd. Keep the ears up; a few quiet words will lift them.
      And shoot at eye level: this is a dog whose dignity depends on being met, not looked down on.
