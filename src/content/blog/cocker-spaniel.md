intro: >-
  <span class="dropcap">T</span>here is a particular look a Cocker Spaniel gives — head slightly
  lowered, eyes raised, ears hanging like drawn curtains — that owners have been failing to describe
  to us for years. <em>Melting</em> is the word they reach for, and then apologise for. They
  needn't. It is a real and specific thing, and rendering it is the whole job of a Cocker portrait.
  The breed is soft in every dimension a painter has to think about: soft of coat, soft of outline,
  soft of expression. The risk is never harshness. The risk is mush. This profile is the three
  styles that hold a Cocker's softness without losing it, and the one that strips it back on
  purpose.

distinctive:
  title: "What makes the Cocker Spaniel visually distinctive."
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      The <em>ears</em> come first, because they are the breed's signature and its trap. Long, low-set,
      and heavily feathered, they hang well below the jaw and frame the face like a pair of drawn
      curtains — and a painter who treats them as a flat brown shape has thrown away the portrait. The
      feathering on a Cocker ear is directional; it falls, it catches light along its length, and it
      moves. We have learned to ask owners for a photograph taken indoors and still, because an ear in
      motion paints as a smudge.
    - >-
      The <em>eyes and expression</em> are the thing being commissioned, whether the owner says so or
      not. Large, dark, set well into a gently domed skull, the Cocker eye carries an expression of
      soft, hopeful attention that the breed wears almost constantly. It is not sad — owners worry that
      it reads as sad — but it is <em>open</em>, entirely without guard, and a good portrait protects
      that openness rather than overstating it into sentimentality.
    - >-
      The <em>coat</em> is the third distinctive thing and the most technical. A Cocker is feathered —
      on the ears, the chest, the belly, the backs of the legs — over a flatter, silkier body coat, and
      the contrast between the two is where a portrait finds its life. The golden coat especially (the
      most common we paint) shifts from honey to deep amber as the feathering thickens. A flat single
      brown is the most common way to get a Cocker wrong.

  analysis:
    - >-
      <em>Watercolour</em> is the recommendation, and for a feathered breed it is almost a foregone
      conclusion. The medium and the coat want the same things: translucency, soft edges, light passing
      through rather than bouncing off. Airy washes catch the honey-to-amber shift of a golden Cocker
      exactly, the feathering dissolves believably into negative space, and the famous soft expression
      survives intact because watercolour never hardens an edge it is not asked to. It is the plate that
      looks most like the dog its owner is picturing.
    - >-
      <em>Oil on Canvas</em> is the traditional answer and a strong one. The Cocker has a direct line
      back into centuries of British sporting portraiture, and oil lends the breed a museum-quality
      warmth — depth in the coat, a settled gravity in the pose — that suits a more formal room. It is
      the plate for a hallway. Watercolour is the plate for the room the family actually lives in.
    - >-
      <em>Renaissance Portrait</em> is the surprise, and it works for a reason owners rarely anticipate:
      the Cocker has genuine gravitas. Given the full Old-Master treatment — sfumato modelling, a
      restrained earth palette, a dark contemplative ground — the breed does not look dressed up. It
      looks dignified, which is a different thing. It is the plate owners least expect to choose and
      most often, in the end, do.
    - >-
      For the fourth plate — and the one we send most often to memorial commissions — see below. A
      Pencil Sketch suits the older Cocker in a way colour, for all its warmth, cannot reach.

photoTips:
  title: "Photo tips specific to Cocker Spaniels."
  plate: { no: "3f", title: "Eye-level three-quarter, ears forward, soft directional light", style: "Recommended angle", variant: 5, breed: floppy, image: "../../assets/blog/cocker-spaniel/photo-tips.jpg", alt: "Reference photograph of a Cocker Spaniel showing the recommended portrait angle and lighting" }
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      The Cocker's photographic problem is the feathering, and it has two halves. The first is light. A
      long feathered coat in hard, direct sun blows out at the tips — the ear edges and chest go to
      white paper, the detail simply gone — while the same coat in flat, shadowless light turns into a
      single soft brown blanket with no feathering at all. The window you want is soft and slightly
      directional: an overcast sky, or open shade, with the light arriving across the dog so the
      feathering keeps both its colour and its fall.
    - >-
      The second half asks something of you before the photograph. <em>Brush the dog, then settle
      it.</em> Feathering photographs as either feathering or as knots, and the camera is unforgiving
      about which. Five minutes with a brush and a few quiet minutes after, and you have a coat that
      paints. Keep the ears forward and visible — a Cocker with its ears swept behind it loses its
      outline — and shoot at eye level, three-quarter on, so the feathering reads down the length of the
      dog.
