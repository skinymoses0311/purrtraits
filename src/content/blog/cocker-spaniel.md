distinctive:
  title: "What makes the Cocker Spaniel visually distinctive."
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      The defining feature is <em>the ears</em>: long, low-set, heavily feathered, and almost always
      the first thing the eye finds. A good Cocker portrait is, in large part, a study of how light falls
      through that feathering. Get the ears right and the dog is unmistakable.
    - >-
      Then there is <em>the expression</em> — the famously soft, melting eye of the gundog, set in a
      round and open face. Where the Collie's eye is intense, the Cocker's is gentle, and the portrait
      has to carry that gentleness without tipping into sentimentality.
    - >-
      And finally <em>the coat</em>: wavy, dense, prone to curl, with feathering on the legs and belly
      that moves when the dog does. It is the most texturally demanding coat we paint, and the reason
      medium matters more for this breed than almost any other.

    - >-
      <em>Watercolour</em> is the recommendation for this breed, and the only breed for which it beats
      the oil. The medium's softness suits the feathering perfectly — washes that bleed at the edges read
      exactly as the soft tips of a Cocker's ears. It is the most flattering plate we offer the breed.
    - >-
      <em>Oil on Canvas</em> comes into its own for the deep solid coats — the blacks, the rich livers —
      where impasto can carry the weight of the coat without losing the wave. For a golden Cocker it can
      read a touch heavy.
    - >-
      <em>Renaissance</em> is the formal plate. The chiaroscuro suits a show dog in full coat, and turns
      the breed's natural softness into something stately. It is the plate most often chosen for a dog
      that has finished its career in the ring.

photoTips:
  title: "Photo tips specific to Cocker Spaniels."
  plate: { no: "13f", title: "Three-quarter, ears forward", style: "Recommended angle", variant: 0, breed: floppy }
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      Photograph the Cocker <em>with the ears forward and the light to one side</em>. The whole charm of
      the breed is in the feathering, and feathering only shows when light rakes across it. Flat light
      turns the ears into a single dark mass.
    - >-
      Catch the dog just after a shake, when the ears settle and the coat is at its fullest. And get
      down to eye level — the soft expression is lost entirely from above, which is exactly where most
      owners point the camera.
