intro: >-
  <span class="dropcap">T</span>he hardest thing about a Springer Spaniel portrait is getting the
  Springer to be in it. The breed is, by design and by temperament, a dog in motion — bred to
  quarter a field all day, built to spring game from cover, and apparently constitutionally unable
  to believe a walk has ended. A portrait, of course, is the opposite of all that: a single, still,
  decided moment. The art of a Springer commission is finding the one second of stillness that still
  has the motion in it — a dog paused, not a dog stopped. This profile is the three styles that hold
  that paused-not-stopped quality, and the one honest trick for catching it with a camera.

distinctive:
  title: "What makes the Springer Spaniel visually distinctive."
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      The <em>frame</em> tells you what the breed is for. A Springer is compact, athletic and powerfully
      built — not heavy, but strong through the shoulder and loin, with the balanced, ready stance of a
      dog that expects to be working within the minute. Even photographed at rest, a good Springer reads
      as <em>coiled</em>: weight slightly forward, attention already elsewhere. A portrait that paints
      the breed as a settled lap dog has painted the wrong animal. The readiness is the likeness.
    - >-
      The <em>coat</em> is the breed's beauty and its technical demand. A Springer wears a moderate,
      weather-resistant coat in a bold particoloured pattern — most often liver-and-white, sometimes
      black-and-white — with feathering on the ears, chest, legs and belly. The pattern is the gift:
      those clean liver patches on white give a painter genuine structure to compose with, a contrast
      the solid breeds never offer. The feathering is the demand: directional, light-catching, and quick
      to read as a smudge if the dog has moved.
    - >-
      The <em>head and expression</em> are pure spaniel — a kind, clean head, long feathered ears set in
      line with the eye, and an expression of warm, eager, trusting attention. The Springer eye is alert
      without an ounce of hardness in it. It is the face of a dog that has decided, on no evidence, that
      the day is going to be wonderful, and a portrait should let it keep that conviction.

  analysis:
    - >-
      <em>Watercolour</em> is the recommendation, and a feathered particoloured breed takes it
      beautifully. Translucent washes handle the liver-and-white pattern with exactly the right softness
      — the patches keep their shape, the feathering dissolves into the paper, and the white of the coat
      becomes the white of the page rather than a laboured highlight. It is the plate that feels most
      like the dog: light, quick, warm, faintly in motion even when it is standing still.
    - >-
      <em>Oil on Canvas</em> is the traditional plate, and the Springer has every right to it. The breed
      sits squarely inside three centuries of British sporting portraiture — the working spaniel at the
      day's end — and oil lends it that inheritance: depth in the coat, weight in the stance, a settled
      museum warmth. It is the plate for the gun-room wall, and it carries the breed's working
      seriousness with no loss of warmth.
    - >-
      <em>Impressionist</em> is the natural third, because the Springer's whole life happens outdoors.
      Monet-style broken light puts the dog back into the field, the hedgerow, the wet morning it was
      bred for — dappled colour, soft edges, a portrait that remembers the work rather than posing for
      it. It is the plate that most owners do not expect to be moved by, and most are.

photoTips:
  title: "Photo tips specific to Springer Spaniels."
  plate: { no: "8f", title: "Eye-level three-quarter, paused after exercise, soft directional light", style: "Recommended angle", variant: 5, breed: floppy, image: "../../assets/blog/springer-spaniel/photo-tips.jpg", alt: "Reference photograph of a Springer Spaniel showing the recommended portrait angle and lighting" }
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      The Springer's photographic problem is not light or coat. It is <em>stillness</em> — there isn't
      any. A Springer photographed at the start of a walk is a liver-and-white blur with a tail
      somewhere in it, and no camera setting will rescue a dog that genuinely will not stop. The honest
      fix is not photographic at all. It is exercise. Photograph a Springer <em>after</em> it has
      properly worked — a good long run, a retrieve or twenty — and you buy yourself a real window of
      stillness: a dog that will stand, settle, and look back at you, just for a moment, with the motion
      still visible in it.
    - >-
      In that window, work quickly and simply. Outdoors, in soft directional light so the feathering
      keeps its detail; eye level, three-quarter on, so the liver patches and the working frame both
      read; and take more than one frame, because the window is short and a Springer's idea of "settled"
      is generous to itself. The shot you want is the dog <em>paused</em> — ears up, weight forward,
      about to be off again — not the rare dog actually at rest.
