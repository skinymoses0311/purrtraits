It is tempting to take one good photo of your dog, decide it is *the* one, and upload it alone. Resist that. A single photo, however lovely, shows the AI your dog from exactly one angle, in one expression, in one moment of light — it is a single guess at who your dog is. A small set of photos is something better: a brief. This guide is about which handful of photos to send, and why three to five beats one every time.

## Why this matters for your portrait

A Purrtraits portrait is generated from the reference photos you upload, and the AI builds its understanding of your dog from whatever those photos contain. From one photo, it learns one view. It does not know what the other side of the face looks like, how the ears sit when they lift, or the true colour of the coat in neutral light rather than that one frame's light.

Give it three to five photos and the picture fills in. Different angles describe the real shape of the head. A close-up pins down the detail of the face; a fuller shot places it on the right body. Several frames of the eyes confirm their true colour and set. The result is a portrait that looks like *your* dog in the round — and a much better chance of getting that likeness right the first time, rather than feeling something is slightly off.

## The four things that matter

### 01 — Send a small set: three to five

One photo is too few; twenty is no better than five. The sweet spot is three to five well-chosen photos.

That is enough to show your dog properly from more than one angle and distance, without burying the good frames among near-duplicates. The diagram shows the idea: a small, deliberate set, each photo earning its place by showing something the others do not. Think of it as briefing the artist, not picking a single favourite.

### 02 — Vary the angle

The most useful variety is angle. If all your photos are dead-on to the dog's face, you have effectively sent one photo several times — and a straight-on shot flattens the head into a mask with no depth.

Include a three-quarter view, where the head turns slightly and you see both eyes plus the line of the muzzle — this is the angle that best describes a dog's real shape. Add a straight front view and a side profile too. Between them, those three angles tell the AI what your dog's head genuinely looks like.

### 03 — Include a close-up and a fuller shot

Vary the distance as well as the angle. One photo should be a close portrait of the face — eyes, expression, the fine detail of the features. Another should be a fuller shot showing the whole dog, head to paws.

The diagram shows the pairing. The close-up gives the portrait its detail; the fuller shot gives it the correct proportions and posture to hang that detail on. One without the other leaves the AI guessing at half the dog.

### 04 — Keep the set consistent

Here is the one rule that ties it together: every photo in the set should be of the same dog, on the same day, in the same kind of light.

It sounds obvious, but mixed sets are common — a sunny photo beside a dim one, a freshly groomed photo beside a scruffy one, last summer's photo beside this winter's heavier coat. Each inconsistency makes the AI's job harder, because the photos disagree about what your dog looks like. Take your set in a single session, in one consistent light, and every photo reinforces the others.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Five near-identical photos.** Variety is the point. Five copies of one angle is still one angle.
- **Mixing photos from different days or seasons.** Coats change. Keep the whole set to one session.
- **One great photo and four poor ones.** Weak frames pull the likeness around. Send only your strong ones.
- **Mixing puppy and adult photos.** Pick one age and stay there — a portrait cannot be both.

## Pre-flight checklist

- Three to five photos, not one.
- A mix of angles — front, three-quarter, profile.
- At least one close-up of the face and one fuller shot.
- All from the same session — same day, same light.
- All sharp — drop any that are not.
- The same dog throughout, at the same age.

**Inline CTA —** Got your set of three to five? That is a proper brief — upload them and see your dog in art.

## Ready to upload?

One photo asks the AI to guess; a small, consistent set lets it understand. Send three to five sharp photos from one session — varied in angle, varied in distance, all the same dog on the same day — and the portrait has the rounded picture it needs. Choose your best set and upload them together.
