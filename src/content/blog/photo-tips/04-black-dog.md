A black dog is the hardest dog to photograph well — and the most rewarding to get right. Black coats swallow light. Photographed carelessly, a black dog comes out as a featureless silhouette: a dog-shaped hole where the face should be. The fix is not a better phone. It is more light, the right exposure, and a little care with the background. Here is how to keep every bit of detail.

## Why this matters for your portrait

A Purrtraits portrait can only render texture it can actually see in your photo. With a pale or tan dog, the camera captures plenty of detail without much help. With a black dog, it does not — and a portrait built from a black silhouette has nothing to work with. It will produce a generic black dog, because that is all the photo gave it.

A black coat is not actually featureless. It has texture, sheen, the play of light along muscle and the soft change of tone around the eyes — all the things that make a portrait look like *your* dog. But that information only survives if you light and expose for it deliberately. Three of the steps below are about light; one is about the single most useful thing your phone can do.

## The four things that matter

### 01 — Give them far more light than feels necessary

A black coat needs roughly twice the light a pale one does to show the same detail. So be generous: position your dog in bright, soft daylight, close to a large window, or outside in open shade on a bright day.

What looks like "enough" light to your eye is usually not enough for a black coat. Err heavily toward bright. If the photo still looks dark, the answer is more light in the room — never the flash, which throws hard, glassy hot-spots across a dark coat and makes things worse.

### 02 — Expose for the dog, not the room

This is the key move, and your phone makes it easy. Left to itself, the camera meters the whole scene, sees a lot of light around a dark dog, and darkens the photo to compensate — taking your dog down with it.

Override it: tap the screen directly on your dog before you shoot. That tells the camera to expose for *them*, and the coat lifts out of the shadow into real detail. The diagram shows the difference one tap makes. On most phones you can then slide a little sun icon to fine-tune — nudge it brighter until the face reads clearly.

### 03 — Light from the side to bring out the texture

Flat, head-on light is a black coat's enemy: it gives the fur nothing to catch, and the dog reads as a flat shape. Side light is the cure. Light raking across the coat from one side traces its texture, models the muscle beneath, and lays a soft sheen along the back that tells the eye "this is fur."

Position your dog so the window is to one side of them, and look for that band of gentle highlight. That sheen is the detail your portrait needs.

### 04 — Never a dark background

A black dog against a dark sofa, a shadowed room or deep foliage simply disappears — the outline dissolves and the AI cannot tell where the dog ends.

Put a black dog against something pale: a light wall, a pale curtain, a bright overcast sky, a sandy path. The contrast snaps the outline into focus and gives the whole portrait a clean edge to build from. With black dogs especially, the background is not a detail — it is half the photo.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Using flash.** On a black coat it produces hard, shiny hot-spots and flat black everywhere else. Soft daylight, always.
- **Trusting auto-exposure.** Without a tap on the dog, the camera will almost always render a black dog too dark.
- **Shooting in a dim room.** "Indoors near a lamp" is nowhere near enough light. Get to a big window.
- **A dark background.** It costs you the outline — the one thing a black dog's portrait most needs.

## Pre-flight checklist

- Bright, soft light — more than feels necessary.
- Tap the screen on your dog to set the exposure.
- Light coming from the side to show coat texture.
- A pale, light background — never dark.
- Check you can see fur and eyes, not a silhouette.
- No flash — it blows hard hot-spots on a dark coat.

## Ready to upload?

Black dogs reward a little extra care more than any other. Give them generous soft light, tap to expose for the dog, light them from the side and set them against something pale — and the silhouette becomes a portrait full of texture and life. Take your best two or three, check the eyes are clearly visible, and upload.
