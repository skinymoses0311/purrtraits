Let us be honest about the goal. You are not going to turn your dog into a statue, and you do not need to. A portrait needs exactly one sharp frame — one second of stillness — and this guide is about engineering that second rather than wishing for it. The trick is not discipline or training. It is setting things up so the calm moment happens on its own, and being ready when it does.

## Why this matters for your portrait

A Purrtraits portrait is built from the detail in your photo, and motion blur destroys detail outright. A dog caught mid-turn is a smear the AI cannot sharpen — there is simply no crisp information in the frame to render from. One blurred photo, however charming the moment, gives a soft, generic portrait.

So you need a single sharp, still frame. The good news is that even the busiest dog produces these constantly — a pause to look at something, the beat before they get up, the moment they settle after a treat. You are not trying to suppress your dog's nature. You are tilting the odds: a calmer dog, a camera ready to fire, and a little patience to wait for the pause that always comes.

## The four things that matter

### 01 — Photograph a tired dog, not a fresh one

This is the whole game, and people skip it. Do not photograph your dog the moment they wake up or just before a walk — that is when they are least able to hold still.

Photograph them *after* exercise. A dog that has had its walk, its run, its play is naturally settled: it will sit, it will hold a pose, it will look at you calmly because it is content to. Twenty minutes of activity beforehand does more for your photo than any amount of asking. A tired dog is a still dog.

### 02 — Put a treat at the lens

A dog will look wherever the treat is. Use that. Hold a treat — or a favourite squeaky toy — just above your phone's lens, and your dog's eyes go exactly where you want them: straight down the camera.

The diagram shows the position. Keep the treat close to the lens, not off to one side, or you will photograph a dog gazing past the camera. Get their attention, hold the treat steady for a beat, and shoot in that moment of focused stillness — then reward them.

### 03 — Brace yourself and shoot a burst

Do not take one photo and hope. Turn on burst or continuous mode — on most phones you simply hold the shutter button down — and fire a run of frames in the still moment. A two-second burst gives you twenty-odd frames; one of them will be sharp.

And hold the phone properly: both hands, elbows tucked into your sides, a steady breath. Half of "blur" is not the dog moving — it is the photographer. A braced phone and a burst together will almost always hand you one clean frame.

### 04 — Catch the pause, don't chase the action

Energetic dogs still pause — to sniff, to listen, to look back at you. Those pauses are your photo. Trying instead to photograph a dog in full motion is a losing game: zoomies and play simply do not produce a usable reference.

So slow down rather than speed up. Settle your dog, get to their eye level, get their attention, and wait. The pause will come. Be ready with your burst when it does — and if you miss it, simply wait for the next one.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Demanding a long "stay".** You need one second, not a minute. Asking for more just creates stress and fidgeting.
- **Photographing before a walk.** The worst possible timing. Tire them out first, every time.
- **No treats, no toy, no helper.** Trying to do it cold makes it ten times harder than it needs to be.
- **Chasing a moving dog.** You cannot out-react the zoomies. Wait for the pause instead.

## Pre-flight checklist

- A walked, settled dog — not a fresh one.
- A treat or toy held right at the lens.
- Burst / continuous mode switched on.
- Both hands on the phone, elbows braced.
- A helper to hold attention, if you have one.
- Several bursts taken — keep only the sharp frames.

**Inline CTA —** Got one crisp, settled frame? That is all a portrait needs — upload it and see your dog in art.

## Ready to upload?

Stillness is not something you force out of a dog; it is something you set up and then wait for. Walk them first, bring treats, shoot a braced burst, and catch the pause rather than the chaos. From a whole burst you only need one sharp frame. Pick your best two or three and upload them.
