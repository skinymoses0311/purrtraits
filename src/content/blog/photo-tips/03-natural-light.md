If you change only one thing about how you photograph your dog, change the light. Not the camera, not the pose, not the background — the light. It is the rawest material a portrait is made from: it decides the true colour of the coat, the depth of every fold and curl, and whether the eyes look alive or flat. The good news is that the best light is free, you already have it, and this guide is simply about learning to see it.

## Why this matters for your portrait

A Purrtraits portrait is generated from your photo, and a photo is, in the end, a record of light. The AI reads light to understand your dog: where the coat catches and where it falls into shadow, what colour the fur really is, the shape the face takes. Give it good light and it has a clear, honest subject to work from. Give it poor light and it is reading guesswork.

This is why light outranks everything else. A perfectly posed dog in bad light makes a poor portrait; an ordinary pose in beautiful light makes a lovely one. And "beautiful light", for our purposes, is not dramatic or clever — it is simply soft, even daylight, coming from the right direction. Once you can recognise it, you will find it in your own home without trying.

## The four things that matter

### 01 — A window beats every bulb you own

Indoors, your light source is a window — ideally a large one, on an overcast day or out of direct sun. Settle your dog within a metre or two of it and the daylight does the work: neutral in colour, soft on the face, bright enough to keep the photo sharp.

Lamps cannot compete. They are dim, they pour yellow over the coat, and they leave most of the dog in shadow. There is no lamp arrangement that beats a plain window. If the room is dark, wait for daylight rather than reaching for a switch.

### 02 — Light from the side, not the front or back

Where the light comes *from* matters as much as how soft it is. Light striking your dog from the side rakes gently across the face and coat, modelling its shape and texture — this is the light you want. Light from directly in front flattens everything into a mask. Light from behind throws the whole face into shadow and kills the catchlight.

The diagram shows the three cases. In practice: stand your dog so the window is to one side of them, then turn them slightly toward it.

### 03 — Soft light, not hard sun

Bright does not mean harsh. Direct midday sun is the trap of outdoor photos — it carves hard black shadows across the face, blows the highlights to white, and makes most dogs squint.

The kindest outdoor light is a bright, overcast day, or a patch of open shade with the sky still wide above it. The cloud, or the shade, acts as one enormous softbox. If the sun is out and strong, move into the shade of a building or a tree and shoot there.

### 04 — Look for the catchlight

The catchlight is the small bright reflection of the light source in your dog's eye. It is tiny, and it is the difference between eyes that look alive and eyes that look like buttons.

You get a catchlight when light reaches the front of the eye — which happens naturally with side or front light from a window, and never with backlight. Before you press the shutter, look closely at the eyes. If you can see two small bright points, the light is working. If the eyes look flat and dark, turn your dog toward the window until they spark.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Putting the window behind the dog.** It feels bright, but it backlights your dog into a silhouette. Keep the window to the side or in front.
- **Mixing daylight and lamplight.** The two colours fight and confuse the coat colour. Switch the lamps off and use the window alone.
- **Shooting at midday outdoors.** The harshest light of the day. Early morning, late afternoon or open shade are far kinder.
- **Brightening a too-dark photo afterwards.** Lifting a dim photo just lifts the noise with it. Get the light right in the room instead.

## Pre-flight checklist

- Daylight only — no bulbs in the mix.
- A large window indoors, or open shade outside.
- Light reaching your dog from the side.
- Bright but soft — overcast, never direct sun.
- A catchlight visible in both eyes.
- Dog turned slightly toward the light.

## Ready to upload?

Good light is not a technique you have to learn so much as a thing you have to notice. Find the soft daylight in your home, put it to the side of your dog, and check for that spark in the eyes. Get the light right and the photo is most of the way there. Take your best two or three and upload them.
