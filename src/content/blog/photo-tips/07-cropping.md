Framing is where a perfectly good photo often gets quietly spoiled at the last moment. People get close — which is right — and then get *too* close, cropping hard against the ears and chin until the portrait has nothing to breathe into. "How close is too close?" is a fair question, and it has a simple answer: close enough to fill the frame, with a clear margin all the way round. This guide is about finding that line and staying on the right side of it.

## Why this matters for your portrait

A Purrtraits portrait needs two things from your framing: enough of your dog, and enough room around them. Enough of the dog means real detail — get close, do not photograph your dog as a small figure in a large room. Enough room means margin — space the portrait can use to compose, reframe and breathe.

The trap is that these pull against each other, and people overcorrect into "too close". Here is the rule that resolves it: a cropped-in photo can always be loosened by the portrait, but detail that was never captured cannot be invented. A clipped ear tip is gone. A chin cut off by the frame edge is gone. Leaving a margin costs you nothing; cropping too tight costs you the very edges of your dog.

## The four things that matter

### 01 — Too tight leaves nothing to work with

A photo cropped brutally close — ears clipped, chin against the edge, the dog crammed corner to corner — gives the portrait no margin at all. There is no room to reframe, and any detail at the frame's edge has been sliced off for good.

Frame your dog's head and chest with a comfortable band of plain background on every side. It should look like there is a little air around them. That air is not wasted space — it is the working room the portrait needs.

### 02 — The safe zone: head, chest, and a margin

There is a reliable frame that works for almost every dog. Picture the head and the top of the chest filling the centre of the photo, with a clear margin of background above the ears, below the chin and out to each side.

The diagram marks this safe zone. Aim for it as your default. It captures all the detail a portrait needs — the face, the eyes, the set of the ears — while keeping the margin that lets the artwork compose properly. Roughly seven dogs in ten fit this frame exactly as drawn.

### 03 — Long and tall dogs need extra room

The exception is the dog whose shape does not fit a tidy square. A Dachshund, a Greyhound, a tall sighthound — frame these as if they were a compact breed and you will run their body straight off the edge.

Give long and tall dogs more room: step back, turn them slightly, and make sure the whole dog — nose to tail, or full standing height — sits inside the frame with space to spare. If your dog is an unusual shape, frame for the *body*, not just the face, and let the margin be generous.

### 04 — Frame it in the camera, not the editor

Do your framing when you take the photo, not afterwards. It is tempting to shoot a wide shot of a small, distant dog and plan to "crop in later" — but cropping in an editor does not add detail, it just enlarges the few pixels your dog occupied, and the result is soft and mushy.

Move your feet until your dog genuinely fills the frame the way you want, then shoot. The diagram shows why a dog framed properly in-camera carries detail that a cropped-down speck never can. Frame once, frame in the camera.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Cropping tight in the editor afterwards.** It enlarges, it does not sharpen. Frame in-camera instead.
- **Anything important touching an edge.** Ears, chin and paws against the frame edge are at risk of being clipped.
- **Shooting a small, distant dog "to crop later".** You cannot crop detail back into existence.
- **Framing a long dog like a compact one.** Their shape needs its own, more generous frame.

## Pre-flight checklist

- Head and chest in frame, with a margin all round.
- Nothing important touching the edges — ears, chin, paws.
- Step back rather than forward when you are unsure.
- Framed in the camera, not the editor afterwards.
- Long or tall dogs given extra room.
- The whole dog sharp — not just the face.

**Inline CTA —** Framed with room to spare? That photo is ready to become art — upload it and see.

## Ready to upload?

Good framing is a balance: close enough for detail, loose enough to leave a margin. Aim for head and chest with air on every side, give the long and tall dogs extra room, and do it all in the camera rather than the editor. Pick your best two or three well-framed shots and upload them.
