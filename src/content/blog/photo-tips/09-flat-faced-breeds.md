Flat-faced breeds — French Bulldogs, Pugs, English Bulldogs and their relatives — photograph differently from other dogs, and a little knowledge goes a long way. Their faces sit on a single shallow plane, their eyes are large and forward-set, and their folds and wrinkles are a defining feature that can read as charming detail or as confusing dark patches, depending entirely on how you light them. This guide covers how to do these wonderful faces justice — and how to keep your dog comfortable while you do.

## Why this matters for your portrait

A Purrtraits portrait works from the structure it can read in your photo. With a long-muzzled dog there is plenty of depth for a camera to describe. With a flat-faced dog there is far less — the face is shallow, so it is easy to flatten it further into a featureless mask, or to lose the folds in shadow.

Two things carry a brachycephalic portrait: the eyes and the folds. The eyes are unusually expressive and prominent — they *are* the face — so they must be sharp. The folds give the face its character — so they must be lit to show as soft, detailed depth rather than black holes. Get those right and the portrait is unmistakably your dog.

One thing first, though, because it matters more than any photo: flat-faced breeds can overheat and tire quickly, and they find heat and exertion harder than other dogs. Keep every session short, calm and cool. A good photo is never worth a distressed dog.

## The four things that matter

### 01 — Shoot straight on, just below the eyeline

Angle is the first decision, and flat faces are unforgiving of a bad one. Photographed from above, a flat-faced dog looks flatter still. Photographed from far below, the nose vanishes entirely.

Position your camera straight on to the face and just slightly below the dog's eyeline. The diagram shows the spot. This angle keeps the proportions honest, gives the forehead and muzzle their proper relationship, and lets those big eyes meet the lens directly.

### 02 — Soft light to read the folds

The folds are the trap. In hard, direct light, every wrinkle fills with a hard black shadow, and the face breaks into confusing dark patches the AI cannot interpret.

Use soft, gentle light — a large window out of direct sun, or open shade outdoors. Soft light settles *into* the folds and describes them as what they are: rounded, detailed depth. You want to see the structure of each wrinkle clearly, with no harsh shadow edge. Soft light from slightly to one side does this best.

### 03 — The eyes are the whole face — get them sharp

On a flat-faced breed, the eyes do almost all the expressive work, and they sit forward on the face. That forward position means the camera will often focus on the nose instead, leaving the eyes slightly soft — a small miss that costs the whole portrait.

Tap the screen directly on an eye to lock focus there, and check the result before moving on. You want both eyes crisp, with a catchlight in each. If the nose is sharp and the eyes are not, take it again.

### 04 — Catch a calm, relaxed expression

Aim for your dog at rest: a soft, settled expression, mouth gently closed or only just open, eyes easy. That is the face you want remembered in a portrait.

Heavy panting is the one to avoid — not only does it photograph as a strained expression, it is also your cue that the dog is getting hot or tired. If you see heavy panting, lolling tongue and tension, stop. Let your dog cool down and rest, and come back to it later. Short, calm sessions give you both a better photo and a happier dog.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Shooting from above.** It exaggerates the flatness of the face. Get straight on and slightly low.
- **Hard light in the folds.** It turns characterful wrinkles into black gaps. Keep the light soft.
- **Focusing on the nose.** The forward eyes are easily missed. Tap to focus on an eye.
- **Pushing a hot or tired dog.** Heavy panting means stop. Comfort comes before the photo, always.

## Pre-flight checklist

- Camera straight on, just below the eyeline.
- Soft light — no hard shadows in the folds.
- Both eyes sharp, with a catchlight in each.
- A calm, settled expression — not mid-pant.
- Sessions kept short; stop if your dog tires or warms up.
- Plain background, head and chest framed.

Flat-faced breeds reward a careful eye: shoot straight on and slightly low, light the folds softly, lock the focus on the eyes, and keep every session short and calm.
