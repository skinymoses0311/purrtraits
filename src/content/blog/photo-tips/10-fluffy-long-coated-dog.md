A long, fluffy coat is the best thing about photographing breeds like Golden Retrievers, Spaniels and the rest of the feathered, fuzzy crowd — and the easiest thing to waste. Done well, a long coat reads as luxurious, flowing, full of light. Done carelessly, the same coat reads as a flat, muddy blur. The difference comes down to three things: how you light it, whether you brushed it, and how sharp the photo is. This guide covers all three.

## Why this matters for your portrait

For a long-coated dog, the coat is not just a detail — it is the headline feature, and a Purrtraits portrait will only render it as well as your photo captures it. The AI needs to see the coat as *fur*: individual strands, the way it falls and feathers, the light moving through it. When it can, the portrait looks rich and tactile. When it cannot, it produces a vague, woolly approximation.

Long fur is also less forgiving than short fur in two specific ways. It shows softness and blur far more obviously — a slightly out-of-focus short coat still looks like a coat, but a slightly soft long coat dissolves into fog. And it shows neglect: tangles and mats that you might not notice in person photograph as messy clumps. A little preparation and care turns the coat from a liability into the star of the portrait.

## The four things that matter

### 01 — Light it from the side or behind

Where the light comes from decides whether a long coat glows or goes dull. Flat light from the front lays the coat down into a single dull tone. Light from the side, or slightly from behind, rakes through the fur — it catches the strands, separates them, and lays a soft luminous edge along the coat.

The diagram shows the directions. Position your dog so soft daylight comes across them or slightly from behind, and watch the coat light up. That glow is the quality you want a portrait to inherit.

### 02 — Brush them first

This is the simplest step and the one most often skipped: groom your dog before the photo. A quick, thorough brush, with any mats teased out, makes an enormous difference.

The camera — and the AI — cannot tell the difference between an artful tangle and a neglected one; both photograph as muddy clumps that break up the flow of the coat. A brushed coat falls cleanly, feathers properly, and reads unmistakably as fur. Five minutes with a brush beforehand does more for the portrait than anything you could do to the photo afterwards.

### 03 — Sharp focus, or the coat turns to fog

Long fur punishes soft focus. Where a short coat tolerates a slightly soft photo, a long coat does not — the fine strands simply dissolve into a haze, and the portrait inherits the fog.

So be exacting about sharpness. Tap the screen to lock focus on your dog, hold the phone steady with both hands, and shoot a burst to be sure of a crisp frame. Then check it properly — zoom into the coat on your screen and confirm you can see individual strands, not a soft blur. If you cannot, take it again.

### 04 — A plain background so the edges read

A long coat has a soft, feathery outline rather than a hard edge — and that delicate edge needs a plain background to be seen against. Place a fluffy dog in front of a busy background and the wispy edges of the coat merge straight into the clutter, and the portrait loses the lovely softness of the silhouette.

Choose a plain background that gently contrasts with the coat — a wall a little darker or lighter than the fur. Against it, every feathered edge stays crisp and separate, and the full shape of the coat reads clearly.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Flat front light.** It flattens a long coat into one dull tone. Light from the side or behind instead.
- **Skipping the brush.** Tangles photograph as mud. A quick groom is the cheapest improvement there is.
- **A slightly soft photo.** Long fur shows blur badly. Insist on sharp focus and check it closely.
- **A busy background.** It swallows the soft, feathery edge of the coat. Keep it plain.

## Pre-flight checklist

- Coat brushed and tangle-free before you start.
- Light coming from the side or slightly behind.
- A plain, gently contrasting background.
- Focus locked on the eyes, the whole dog sharp.
- Soft daylight — no flat front light, no flash.
- Your dog dry and settled.
