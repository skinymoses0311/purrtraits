Here is the short answer, so you can stop worrying about it: the phone in your pocket is good enough. You do not need to borrow a "proper camera", and you certainly do not need to buy one. Every reference photo we would call excellent was taken on a phone. What separates a good reference photo from a poor one is not the device — it is light, focus and framing, all of which a modern phone handles well. This guide is about what actually matters, and the one setting worth changing.

## Why this matters for your portrait

We mention this because gear anxiety is a real reason people put off ordering a portrait — they assume their phone photos "won't be good enough" and wait for a camera that never materialises. They are wrong to wait.

A Purrtraits portrait is generated from the information in your photo: the shape of the face, the colour and texture of the coat, sharp eyes, a clean outline. A phone from the last several years captures all of that in abundance. A dedicated camera in skilled hands can do a little more, but it cannot do anything the AI actually needs that a phone cannot. What a camera *cannot* do is fix bad light or a careless angle — and those are the things that genuinely decide a portrait. Spend your effort there, not on equipment.

## The four things that matter

### 01 — Light beats megapixels, every time

If you put a well-lit phone photo next to a badly-lit photo from an expensive camera, the phone photo wins — easily. Resolution is not the bottleneck; light is.

A good camera in poor light still produces a dull, murky image. A modest phone in soft daylight produces a bright, detailed one. So stop thinking about the device and think about the window. Get your dog into good light and your phone has everything it needs.

### 02 — Lock the focus and exposure

The one place a phone genuinely needs your help is deciding *what* to focus on — left alone it may lock onto the background. Fix this in a second: tap and hold on your dog's face on the screen until it confirms the focus and exposure are locked.

Now the dog stays sharp and correctly lit even if they shift slightly, and even if something moves behind them. The diagram shows the gesture. It is the single most useful thing your phone's camera can do, and most people never use it.

### 03 — Turn off portrait mode

This is the setting to change. Phone "portrait mode" artificially blurs the background to imitate a camera lens — and for a reference photo, that is the opposite of helpful.

The effect works by guessing where the dog ends, and it guesses badly around fur: ear tips, stray whiskers and the soft edge of the coat get smudged and haloed. That is exactly the fine detail a portrait needs sharp. Shoot in standard photo mode, where the whole dog stays crisp and honest. Let the artwork handle the styling — your photo should be plain and accurate.

### 04 — Move your feet, don't use the zoom

When you want your dog larger in the frame, walk towards them. Do not pinch to zoom.

Digital zoom does not magnify detail — it crops into the photo and enlarges what is left, throwing away resolution and adding mush. The diagram shows the difference. Closing the distance with your feet keeps every pixel of real detail. Get within a couple of metres and fill the frame the honest way.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Waiting for a "real camera".** Your phone is ready now. Do not let imaginary gear delay the portrait.
- **Using digital zoom.** It quietly destroys the detail you are trying to capture. Walk closer.
- **Leaving portrait mode on.** It smudges the fur edges the portrait most needs. Standard mode, always.
- **Blaming the phone for a dim photo.** It is the light that is short, not the device. Find a window.

## Pre-flight checklist

- Use the phone you already own — it is enough.
- Tap and hold to lock focus and exposure on your dog.
- Portrait mode OFF — no fake background blur.
- Move closer with your feet, never the zoom.
- Good daylight — a phone needs it even more than a camera does.
- Lens wiped clean before you start.

**Inline CTA —** Phone in hand, portrait mode off? You are fully equipped — upload your photo and see.

## Ready to upload?

The best camera for your dog's portrait is the one already in your pocket. Give it good light, lock the focus, switch portrait mode off and move closer with your feet — do that and a phone photo will carry a portrait beautifully. Take your best two or three and upload them.
