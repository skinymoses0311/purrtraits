When a pet portrait comes back looking *almost* right — a little generic, a little off, not quite your dog — the cause is rarely the artwork. It's the photo it was built from. And a weak reference photo tends to fail in one of five specific ways. None of them is about skill or equipment. Each is free to fix and takes seconds. Here are the five mistakes that wreck a portrait, and exactly how to avoid them.

## Why this matters for your portrait

Your portrait is generated from the reference photo you upload. The AI reads that photo for the shape of the face, the colour of the coat, the catchlight in the eyes — and it renders what it finds. It does not flatter, correct or fill in gaps. What you photograph is what it works from.

Each of the five mistakes below removes something the AI needs. A lamp-lit photo hands it the wrong coat colour. A photo shot from above hands it the wrong proportions. A blurred photo hands it no fine detail at all. The AI cannot recover information the photo never captured — so a portrait built on a flawed photo inherits every one of its flaws.

The encouraging part: these are not subtle errors that need a trained eye. They are five blunt, common habits, and once you can name them you will not make them again. Four of the five are settled before you even press the shutter.

## The five mistakes

### 01 — Shooting under artificial light

The most common mistake of all. A photo taken under a ceiling light, or with the flash on, looks fine on the phone screen and wrong everywhere else. Household bulbs cast a heavy yellow-orange tint that the AI reads as your dog's actual coat colour — so a cool grey turns muddy brown. Flash flattens the face and bounces straight back off the eyes.

The fix costs nothing: photograph your dog in daylight, near a window or outside in open shade. Daylight is neutral, even and kind to a coat. Turn the flash off and leave it off.

### 02 — Standing over the dog

Most people photograph their dog from their own standing height, phone angled down. It feels natural. It distorts everything.

From above, a dog becomes a large head, a foreshortened body and very little face. Drop to their eye level — kneel, crouch, sit on the floor — and the proportions correct themselves: two eyes, a full muzzle, a real expression. The diagram shows how much the angle changes. This single mistake is behind more disappointing portraits than any other.

### 03 — The background you stopped noticing

You see your dog. You do not see the laundry pile, the table leg, or the houseplant directly behind their head — but the camera records all of it, and the AI tries to make sense of all of it.

A cluttered background does not just distract; it actively confuses the generation, blurring the line between dog and surroundings. Move your dog in front of something plain — a wall, a curtain, a clear patch of floor or grass — and look properly at the whole frame before you shoot, not only at the dog.

### 04 — Cropping the life out of it

A photo cropped hard against the ears, the chin or the chest leaves the portrait with nothing to work with — no margin to reframe, no room to compose.

Frame your dog's head and chest with a clear band of background on every side. The diagram marks the safe zone. If you are unsure, take a step back: a slightly loose photo can always be cropped in, but a chin that was cut off was never captured and cannot be added.

### 05 — Sending a filtered or edited photo

This one feels counter-intuitive, which is exactly why it catches people out: do not "improve" the photo before you upload it. Filters, beauty modes, heavy brightening, fake background blur — they all rewrite the very information the AI needs to read accurately.

A filter shifts the coat colour. Over-sharpening invents texture that was never there. Phone portrait mode can soften the edge of the dog itself. Send the photo straight off the camera, untouched. The artwork is the creative step — your job is an honest record of your dog.

## A few smaller ones

Beyond the big five, four habits that quietly cost you detail:

- **A dirty lens.** Phone lenses live in pockets. A quick wipe before you shoot clears a haze you may not even notice on screen.
- **Zooming instead of moving.** Digital zoom throws detail away. Walk closer instead.
- **Shooting through glass or a screen door.** It adds reflections and a colour cast. Open the door.
- **Photographing a wet or just-bathed dog.** A flat, damp coat does not represent your dog — wait until they are dry and settled.

## Pre-flight checklist

- Daylight only — flash off, ceiling light off.
- Camera down at your dog's eye level.
- Background plain and quiet.
- Head and chest framed with a clear margin.
- Straight off the camera — no filters, no portrait mode.
- Lens wiped clean before you start.

**Inline CTA —** Avoided all five? Then you have a reference photo worth building on. Upload it and see the difference.

## Ready to upload?

Every one of these five mistakes is free to fix and takes seconds — and avoiding them is most of what separates a portrait that looks like *a* dog from one that looks like *yours*. Check your photo against the list, pick your best two or three, and upload. The artwork takes it from there.
