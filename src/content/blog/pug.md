intro: >-
  <span class="dropcap">T</span>he Pug has the most openly comic face of any breed we paint, and —
  this is the part owners enjoy — it is also the breed that most rewards being taken completely
  seriously. There is a joke available in every Pug portrait, and a renderer left to itself will
  tell it: the bug eyes, the flat face, the curl of tail, all dialled up into a cartoon. The better
  portrait does the opposite. It treats the Pug as a sitter — a small, dignified, faintly theatrical
  sitter — and lets the comedy arrive on its own, the way it does with the living dog. This profile
  is the three styles that hold that balance, including the one that grants a Pug the full gravity
  of an Old Master.

distinctive:
  title: "What makes the Pug visually distinctive."
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      The <em>face</em> is the entire breed, and it is a remarkable piece of design. Round, flat, and
      frontal, the Pug face is organised by deep wrinkles — the famous brow folds, the rolls across the
      short muzzle — set around a pair of large, round, dark eyes and a neat underbite. No breed packs
      more expression into less space. For a painter this is a concentration problem: everything that
      matters happens in a small frontal disc, and every fold either carries the picture or clutters it.
    - >-
      The <em>eyes</em> are where the Pug stops being funny. They are large, dark, lustrous and
      astonishingly expressive — capable, on the same afternoon, of comedy, reproach, and a soft soulful
      melancholy that owners find genuinely affecting. The whole argument for painting a Pug seriously
      rests on those eyes. A portrait that exaggerates them into googly cartoon eyes has thrown the dog
      away; a portrait that paints them honestly has found the small, real animal behind the famous
      mask.
    - >-
      The <em>build</em> completes the picture. The Pug is compact and cobby — a square, sturdy little
      dog — with a short coat, most often fawn with a black mask, and a tightly curled tail carried high
      over the hip. The body asks little of a painter; like the Pug's flat-faced cousins, the coat steps
      back so the face can do everything. The curled tail is the one flourish, and it is worth getting
      exactly right.

  analysis:
    - >-
      <em>Pop Art</em> is the recommendation, and the Pug face is almost custom-built for it. The
      breed's whole personality lives in a flat, bold, frontal arrangement of features, and silkscreen
      colour amplifies every part of it — the wrinkles become graphic shapes, the dark eyes become
      anchors, the curled tail becomes a punctuation mark. It is the plate that makes a Pug owner grin
      on sight, and the one that travels best onto a small format or a phone screen.
    - >-
      <em>Comic Book</em> is the warmer companion. Inked outlines give the round face genuine structure,
      and cel-shaded colour keeps the strong contrast while leaving the eyes their range. Where Pop Art
      turns the Pug into a graphic, Comic Book keeps it a character — a small expressive one,
      mid-thought, with that flicker of melancholy intact beneath the comedy.
    - >-
      <em>Renaissance Portrait</em> is the surprise, and it is, frankly, the most delightful plate we
      offer for any breed. Give a Pug the full Old-Master treatment — sfumato modelling, a restrained
      earth palette, a dark contemplative ground, the lighting of a court portrait — and the result is
      not a joke. Or rather: it is a joke and a genuinely beautiful portrait at the same time, and
      refuses to choose. The formality and the face strike a contrast that owners find irresistible, and
      it is the plate most often commissioned as a gift.

photoTips:
  title: "Photo tips specific to Pugs."
  plate: { no: "9f", title: "Slight three-quarter, eye level, soft even frontal light", style: "Recommended angle", variant: 5, breed: floppy, image: "../../assets/blog/pug/photo-tips.jpg", alt: "Reference photograph of a Pug showing the recommended portrait angle and lighting" }
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      The Pug shares the flat-faced photographic problem of the French and English Bulldogs, in its most
      concentrated form. Photographed dead-on, the Pug's flat face loses every scrap of depth — it
      records as a flat disc, the muzzle simply gone — and the deep wrinkles, in hard light, fill with
      hard shadow that turns expression into grime. The fix is the same small, reliable one: turn the
      dog a few degrees off square. A slight three-quarter gives the short muzzle a hint of dimension
      while the face stays frontal enough to read.
    - >-
      Light should be soft and roughly frontal — an overcast window, a bright even room — so it fills
      the wrinkles gently and keeps the eyes the brightest, clearest things in the frame. The eyes are
      the whole portrait; protect them. And get down to the dog: a Pug is a small dog, and a photograph
      taken from standing height looms over a foreshortened, all-forehead version of it. Kneel, meet the
      dog at its own eyeline, and the real, expressive little face is right there waiting.
