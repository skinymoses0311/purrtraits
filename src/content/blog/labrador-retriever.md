intro: >-
  <span class="dropcap">T</span>he Labrador is the most-commissioned dog in our studio, and for our
  first two years it was also the one we most often got wrong. Not because the breed is difficult —
  because it isn't. There is no dramatic mask to balance, no improbable silhouette, no single
  arresting feature to build a portrait around; a Labrador hands a painter almost nothing to hide
  behind. What it hands over instead is an expression — open, hopeful, faintly anxious to please —
  and a coat that runs one colour from nose to tail. This profile is what two years and several
  thousand Labradors taught us: the three plates we now recommend, the light that keeps a solid coat
  from going flat, and why this is the portrait most often bought for the people rather than the
  dog.

distinctive:
  title: "What makes the Labrador Retriever visually distinctive."
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      Three things carry a Labrador on canvas, and the first is the <em>coat</em>. It is the breed's
      great difficulty: a single, solid colour — yellow, black, or chocolate — with no markings, no
      blaze, no contrast to lean on. Where a Border Collie hands the painter an asymmetric mask to
      balance against, a Labrador hands over one tone and expects it to be made interesting. It can be.
      The Labrador coat is a double coat, dense and faintly oiled, and in good light it is never
      actually one colour — there is warm umber in the shadow of a yellow dog, and a surprising
      blue-grey in the deep black of a black one. The whole portrait lives in that modulation.
    - >-
      The second is the <em>expression</em>. The Labrador head is broad and kind, the eyes set wide and
      warm brown, and the breed wears its mood with no discretion whatsoever. There is a particular
      Labrador look — ears lifted a half-inch, brow faintly creased, mouth just open — that owners
      describe, without fail, as <em>hopeful</em>. It is the thing they are commissioning, whether they
      know it or not. We have learned to ask for a photograph taken in the moment before a meal, or a
      walk, or a thrown ball: that is when the look arrives.
    - >-
      The third is the <em>build</em> — and in particular the tail. A Labrador stands four-square and
      substantial, balanced rather than coiled, a dog plainly comfortable in a room. But it is the tail
      that signs the portrait: thick at the base, tapering, carried in a low confident curve — the
      famous <em>otter tail</em>, the one feature of the breed that is genuinely hard to draw and
      impossible to fake. A portrait that gets the tail right is recognised across a hallway. A portrait
      that gets it wrong is, very politely, never mentioned again.

  analysis:
    - >-
      <em>Oil on Canvas</em> is the recommendation, and for the Labrador it is barely a contest. The
      breed walked straight out of three centuries of British sporting portraiture — the gun dog at the
      keeper's heel, painted in oil, hung in a hall — and the medium still carries that inheritance. Oil
      gives the solid coat the one thing it most needs: depth. Layered glaze over a warm ground turns a
      flat yellow into something with umber in its shadows and light along its spine. It is the plate we
      send to most Labrador owners, and the one that, fifty years on, will still look like the dog.
    - >-
      <em>Watercolour</em> is the lighter answer, and a genuinely good one. The breed's coat — yellow
      especially, but chocolate too — takes an airy wash beautifully; the medium catches the slight
      sheen of the double coat in a way that reads as <em>health</em> rather than gloss. It forgives a
      younger dog whose frame has not yet filled out, and it suits the Labradors commissioned as a gift
      rather than a memorial — there is something hopeful in the paper left showing through. For a
      yellow Lab under two, it is the plate we would gently steer you toward.
    - >-
      <em>Impressionist</em> is the surprise on the list, and the one owners least expect to choose
      until they see it. The Labrador is a dog of <em>places</em> — a beach, a stubble field, the muddy
      margin of a river — and Monet-style broken light puts the dog back into that world without
      painting a literal background. Dappled colour, soft edges, a portrait that remembers the walk. It
      is not the plate for a formal hall. It is the plate for the kitchen wall — and a great many
      kitchens are where Labradors are actually loved.
    - >-
      For the fourth plate — and the one we send most often to memorial commissions — see below. A
      Pencil Sketch suits the older Labrador in a way the colour mediums, for all their warmth, cannot
      quite reach.

  title: "Photo tips specific to Labrador Retrievers."
  plate: { no: "1f", title: "Eye-level three-quarter, soft raking light", style: "Recommended angle", variant: 5, breed: floppy, image: "../../assets/blog/labrador-retriever/photo-tips.jpg", alt: "Reference photograph of a Labrador Retriever showing the recommended portrait angle and lighting" }
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      The Labrador's photographic problem is the coat, and it is the same problem in three colours. A
      solid coat needs <em>light that has direction</em>. Flat midday sun erases a yellow Labrador's
      form entirely — the dog becomes a pale shape with eyes — while the same light turns a chocolate
      coat muddy and loses a black Labrador altogether, every contour swallowed. The fix is not a better
      camera. It is softer, raking light: an overcast sky, or the hour after sunrise, with the light
      arriving across the dog rather than straight down on it. That is the moment a single colour
      becomes a hundred.
    - >-
      The second instruction is harder to follow, because it asks something of the dog. Photograph a
      Labrador <em>after</em> exercise, not before. The breed's resting state is motion; a Lab fresh out
      of the door is a blur with a tail. Twenty minutes of a thrown ball, then a few quiet minutes, and
      you have a dog that will hold a settled three-quarter stance — body angled, head turned back to
      you, that hopeful look arriving right on cue. Eye level, always: kneel to the dog's height. A
      Labrador photographed from above looks apologetic. Photographed from its own eyeline, it looks
      like itself.
