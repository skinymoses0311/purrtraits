intro: >-
  <span class="dropcap">T</span>he French Bulldog is the easiest dog in our catalogue to make iconic
  and the hardest to make tender. Everything about the breed is graphic — the bat ears standing to
  attention, the flat domino face, the eyes set wide and dark — and a renderer left to itself will
  hand you a sticker. A good sticker, but a sticker. What that first pass misses is the thing
  Frenchie owners are actually attached to: the small anxious softness behind the comedy, the faint
  sense that the dog suspects it has said the wrong thing. This profile is about holding both at
  once — the logo and the worry — across three styles built for bold faces, and a fourth built for
  none.

distinctive:
  title: "What makes the French Bulldog visually distinctive."
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      Begin with the <em>ears</em>, because everyone else does. The bat ear — broad at the base, rounded
      at the tip, carried bolt upright — is the single most reproduced feature of any breed we paint. It
      is also a gift to portraiture: it frames the face, it catches light along its rim, and it gives an
      otherwise compact head a strong, symmetrical silhouette. Get the ears wrong and nothing else
      matters. Get them right and the portrait is already half-recognised.
    - >-
      The <em>face</em> itself is built like a graphic device. It is flat, near-symmetrical, organised
      into clean zones — the dark mask, the wide nose, the pale muzzle — with a single expressive
      wrinkle above the nose doing the work of a whole brow. The eyes are round, wide-set and dark, and
      they are where the breed's real expression lives: not comic at all, but watchful, faintly hopeful,
      easily hurt. A portrait that paints only the comedy has painted half a dog.
    - >-
      The <em>build</em> is the quiet part. A Frenchie is cobby and low and surprisingly heavy, a small
      dog with a serious frame, and the short fine coat — fawn, brindle, or pied — carries almost no
      detail of its own. That is the breed's portrait logic in a sentence: the coat steps back so the
      face can do everything. Every style we recommend is, in the end, a style for a face.

  analysis:
    - >-
      <em>Pop Art</em> is the recommendation, and for this breed it is almost too easy. The Frenchie
      face is already a piece of graphic design — flat planes, hard symmetry, a built-in mask — and bold
      silkscreen colour simply turns the volume up. Saturated blocks, a two- or three-colour palette, a
      halftone hum: the bat ears and wide eyes become the poster they were always trying to be. It is
      the plate that makes people laugh out loud, and the one most often hung where guests will see it.
    - >-
      <em>Comic Book</em> is the close second, and the warmer of the two bold options. Inked outlines of
      varying weight give the face structure without flattening it, and cel-shaded colour keeps the
      character while leaving room — in the eyes, in the wrinkle — for that watchful softness to
      survive. If Pop Art is the Frenchie as a brand, Comic Book is the Frenchie as a character with a
      small inner life.
    - >-
      <em>Geometric</em> is the modern outlier and the one owners pick when they want the portrait to
      sit in a contemporary room rather than a joke. The compact, planar face translates unusually well
      into faceted low-poly form — the planes were half there already — and the result is clean,
      sculptural, and quietly stylish. It is the least funny plate, deliberately.
    - >-
      For the fourth plate — and the one we send most often to memorial commissions — see below. A
      Pencil Sketch finds the part of the breed the bold styles are built to skip.

photoTips:
  title: "Photo tips specific to French Bulldogs."
  plate: { no: "2f", title: "Slight three-quarter, eye level, soft frontal light", style: "Recommended angle", variant: 5, breed: pointy, image: "../../assets/blog/french-bulldog/photo-tips.jpg", alt: "Reference photograph of a French Bulldog showing the recommended portrait angle and lighting" }
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      The French Bulldog's photographic problem is its best feature: the flat face. Photographed
      straight on, a brachycephalic face has almost no depth for a camera to find — the muzzle
      disappears, the wide nose dominates, and the dog flattens into a mask of itself. The fix is small
      and counter-intuitive. Turn the dog just a few degrees off square — a slight three-quarter, no
      more — and the muzzle regains a hint of dimension while the bat ears stay symmetrical enough to
      read. It is the difference between a passport photo and a portrait.
    - >-
      Light matters less here than it does for a long-coated breed, but it matters in one specific way:
      the folds. Hard side-light drops a hard shadow into the nose-wrinkle and the corners of the mouth,
      ageing the dog and souring the expression. Soft, roughly frontal light — an overcast window, a
      bright room — fills those folds and lets the eyes stay the brightest thing in the frame. Eye
      level, always. A Frenchie photographed from above becomes all forehead; photographed from its own
      eyeline, it becomes itself.
