intro: >-
  <span class="dropcap">M</span>ost breeds are commissioned for a face. The Golden Retriever is
  commissioned for a coat. It is the first thing an owner describes, usually with their hands — the
  weight of it, the way it lifts in wind, the specific warm colour it turns in late afternoon — and
  it is the thing a portrait has to get right before anything else can matter. That sounds simple.
  It is not. <em>Gold</em> is a narrow target: a degree too pale and the dog reads as a yellow
  Labrador, a degree too warm and it reads as muddy brown. This profile is the three styles that
  find the gold, the light that protects it, and the gentle open face the coat is wrapped around.

distinctive:
  title: "What makes the Golden Retriever visually distinctive."
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      The <em>coat</em> is the breed and the brief. A Golden carries a dense, water-repellent double
      coat with generous feathering — on the tail, the legs, the chest, the back of the thighs — and a
      colour that runs anywhere from pale cream to deep lustrous gold. Crucially, it is never one colour
      at once: there is light along the spine and the crown of the head, deeper amber in the feathering
      and the shadow of the legs. A portrait that paints a Golden as a single flat tone has painted a
      cushion. The whole picture lives in that gradient.
    - >-
      The <em>expression</em> is the breed's second gift. The Golden head is broad and kind, the muzzle
      straight, the eyes dark, warm and set well into the skull — and the breed wears, more reliably
      than almost any dog we paint, an expression of open, uncomplicated friendliness. Owners call it a
      smile, and while we are careful never to paint a literal one, the openness is real and it is what
      the commission is for. The eyes do the smiling.
    - >-
      The <em>build</em> is athletic and balanced — a working gundog under all that coat, substantial
      without being heavy, with a level topline and a feathered tail carried in a confident, level
      sweep. The frame matters because it keeps the feathering honest: a Golden is a powerful dog
      dressed in a soft coat, and a portrait should never let the softness forget the strength
      underneath.

  analysis:
    - >-
      <em>Watercolour</em> is the recommendation, and for this breed it is the obvious one done well.
      The golden coat and the medium want the same thing: light passing <em>through</em> the colour
      rather than sitting on top of it. Layered translucent washes catch the cream-to-gold gradient
      exactly, the feathering melts believably into the paper, and the warmth stays warm without ever
      curdling into brown. It is the plate that looks the way owners describe the dog when they are not
      trying.
    - >-
      <em>Impressionist</em> is the close and natural companion. The Golden is a creature of the
      outdoors — a riverbank, a stubble field, a long evening walk — and Monet-style broken light puts
      the dog back into that world without painting a literal scene behind it. Dappled colour and soft
      edges treat the coat as something the light is <em>moving through</em>. It is the plate for a
      kitchen wall, and the one that most feels like a particular afternoon.
    - >-
      <em>Oil on Canvas</em> is the traditional plate, and a confident one. The breed's sporting
      heritage gives oil an immediate cultural fit — depth in the coat, a settled gravity, the warmth of
      a hall portrait — and it suits owners who want the commission to read as formal and permanent.
      Watercolour is the dog you walk with; oil is the dog on the wall when you come home.
    - >-
      For the fourth plate — and the one we send most often to memorial commissions — see below.

photoTips:
  title: "Photo tips specific to Golden Retrievers."
  plate: { no: "6f", title: "Eye-level three-quarter, outdoors, soft low evening light", style: "Recommended angle", variant: 5, breed: floppy, image: "../../assets/blog/golden-retriever/photo-tips.jpg", alt: "Reference photograph of a Golden Retriever showing the recommended portrait angle and lighting" }
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      The Golden's photographic problem is the gold itself. In flat, shadowless light a golden coat dies
      — the gradient collapses, the warmth drains, and a bright dog photographs as a dull khaki blur. In
      hard midday sun the opposite happens: the highlights along the back and head blow out to bare
      white, and the feathering loses every strand. The coat needs <em>soft, low, directional</em> light
      — an overcast sky, or the golden hour an hour before sunset — arriving across the dog so the
      colour grades from lit gold to amber shadow the way it does in life.
    - >-
      The practical instructions are two. Photograph outdoors where the light has room to be generous,
      and brush the dog first — feathering reads as feathering or as a tangle, and the camera will not
      forgive a knot. Then get to the dog's eye level, three-quarter on, ideally a few quiet minutes
      after a walk so the open, settled expression has arrived. A Golden at eye level in good evening
      light very nearly paints itself.
