intro: >-
  <span class="dropcap">T</span>he English Bulldog is the most recognisable dog in the country and,
  for a portrait studio, one of the most quietly difficult. The difficulty is not the folds, the
  jowls, or the famous undershot jaw — those render beautifully once you know how. It is the tone.
  The Bulldog has a comic face that the dog itself treats with complete seriousness, and a portrait
  has to take the same view: never laughing <em>at</em> the breed, but not pretending the comedy
  isn't there either. Get the tone wrong and you have painted a cartoon, or a sad dog. Get it right
  and you have painted the truth, which is a deadpan one. This profile is the three styles that hold
  that line.

distinctive:
  title: "What makes the English Bulldog visually distinctive."
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      The <em>head</em> is the breed, and it is enormous. Broad, deep, set low between heavy shoulders,
      the Bulldog head is organised entirely by skin — the brow folds, the rope of wrinkle across the
      muzzle, the jowls hanging in soft heavy curtains either side of the undershot jaw. For a painter
      this is not a problem; it is a gift. Skin in folds gives a portrait structure, shadow, and weight,
      and the Bulldog has more of it than any breed we paint.
    - >-
      The <em>expression</em> lives where you least expect it: in the eyes, set wide and low and
      frequently half-hidden in the brow folds, looking out with an expression that is not comic at all.
      It is patient, faintly stoic, occasionally melancholic — the real dog behind the famous face. A
      portrait that finds those eyes and lets them carry the picture has understood the breed. A
      portrait that paints only the jaw has painted a mask.
    - >-
      The <em>build</em> is low, wide, and deliberate — a dog with a serious centre of gravity, a
      wide-fronted stance, and a short fine coat (most often pied, fawn, or red-and-white) that carries
      almost no detail of its own. The small rose ears fold neatly back, contributing nothing and asking
      for nothing. As with the Bulldog's French cousin, the coat steps back so the head can do all the
      work.

  analysis:
    - >-
      <em>Pop Art</em> is the recommendation, and the Bulldog face is almost embarrassingly suited to
      it. Wrinkles, jowls, and an underbite are exactly the kind of bold, legible iconography silkscreen
      colour was built to amplify — flat saturated blocks, hard outlines, a face turned into a graphic
      without losing a single fold. It is the plate that makes the breed's deadpan land as deadpan, and
      it is the one most often hung where it will get a reaction.
    - >-
      <em>Comic Book</em> is the warmer companion. Inked outlines of varying weight give the heavy head
      genuine structure, and cel-shaded colour keeps the character while leaving the eyes room to do
      their stoic, patient work. Where Pop Art turns the Bulldog into an icon, Comic Book keeps it a
      character — one with a long-suffering inner life you can almost read.

photoTips:
  title: "Photo tips specific to English Bulldogs."
  plate: { no: "4f", title: "Slight three-quarter, eye level, soft frontal light", style: "Recommended angle", variant: 5, breed: floppy, image: "../../assets/blog/english-bulldog/photo-tips.jpg", alt: "Reference photograph of a English Bulldog showing the recommended portrait angle and lighting" }
  paragraphs:
    - >-
      The Bulldog's photographic problem is, like the French Bulldog's, the flat face — but with an
      added complication: the folds. Photographed straight on in hard light, a Bulldog flattens
      <em>and</em> sours: the muzzle loses depth, and every brow fold and jowl crease fills with a hard
      black shadow that ages the dog and turns its patient expression into a scowl. The breed needs
      soft, roughly frontal light — an overcast sky, open shade, a bright window — which fills the folds
      gently and keeps the eyes the brightest point in the frame.
    - >-
      For angle, turn the dog a few degrees off square. A slight three-quarter gives the great head some
      genuine dimension and lets the jowls read as the heavy, three-dimensional things they are, rather
      than as a flat outline. Eye level is non-negotiable: a Bulldog photographed from above becomes all
      skull and no eyes, and the eyes are the entire portrait. Kneel to the dog. It will not come up to
      you.
